# S0011: GF Cross-Tissue Multi-Omics

Metadata:
- Title: `Cross-tissue multi-omics analyses reveal the gut microbiota's absence impacts organ morphology, immune homeostasis, bile acid and lipid metabolism.`
- Journal/year/DOI: iMeta, 2025, `10.1002/imt2.272`
- Code: `https://github.com/BGI-Intestines/Germ-free-mice`; `https://github.com/BGIResearch/SAW`; `https://github.com/MGI-tech-bioinformatics/DNBelab_C_Series_HT_scRNA-analysis-software`

Story layer:
- Central question: What systemic tissue microenvironment, immune and metabolic changes follow microbiota absence?
- Biological gap: GF effects are distributed across organs, not confined to gut tissue.
- Technical gap: Requires cross-tissue scRNA, Stereo-seq, aggregation indices, spatial localization and metabolomics.
- One-sentence story: A cross-tissue multi-omics atlas shows that microbiota deficiency changes immune compartments, organ spatial organization, intestinal epithelial zonation, lipid programs and bile-acid metabolism.
- Archetype: cross_tissue_atlas; multiomics_condition.
- Final claim type: multi-organ condition atlas with spatial and metabolic support.

Figure logic:
- Fig. 1: study overview, multi-omics design, major cell types, tissue immune counts, spatial cell/gene maps and aggregation index.
- Fig. 2: B-cell compartment heterogeneity across tissues and GF/SPF differences, with spleen spatial validation.
- Fig. 3: myeloid and NK/T cell compartments, neutrophil/T-cell programs and spatial marker maps.
- Fig. 4: intestinal epithelial zonation, cell-type enrichment in zones, GO and metabolic pathway activity.
- Fig. 5: lipid absorption/metabolism and liver zonation of the ZBTB20-LPL axis, with IF.
- Fig. 6: bile acid concentration and synthesis/reabsorption capacity across body sites.

Detailed figure reading:
- Fig. 1 is the cross-tissue overview. It introduces GF/SPF multi-omics, summarizes differential phenotypes, annotates major cell types across tissues, reports immune-cell counts, shows spatial marker maps for thymus, spleen and liver zonation, and defines a BFS-based cell aggregation index. The figure's job is to prove this is a systemic atlas, not a single-organ story. For reuse, cross-tissue papers need both a global cell atlas and at least a few spatial examples that show organ-specific structure.
- Fig. 2 focuses the systemic story on B cells. It subclusters B cells, compares cell cycle and subtype proportions across tissues, uses heatmaps for GF/SPF shifts, performs GO on marginal-zone and non-marginal-zone B cells, and validates spleen B-cell organization with Stereo-seq genes. For reuse, this is an immune-compartment module: cross-tissue subtype map -> condition shift -> pathway interpretation -> one spatial validation tissue.
- Fig. 3 repeats the immune logic for myeloid and NK/T cells. It shows UMAPs, subtype proportions, cross-tissue heatmaps, neutrophil GO, spleen spatial marker maps, Ctla4 localization in white pulp, CD8 alpha-alpha DEG/enrichment and inflammatory cytokine fold-change summaries. The figure supports immune-homeostasis disruption across innate and lymphoid compartments. For reuse, use repeated immune modules only when each adds a distinct biological axis; otherwise merge them.
- Fig. 4 zooms into intestinal epithelial zonation. Stereo-seq defines intestinal zones, cell-type enrichment is quantified by zone, epithelial compartments are compared, segment DEGs and GO are summarized, scMetabolism scores metabolic pathways and Reg3b/Reg3g spatial maps validate innate-defense changes. For reuse, this is the template for layered/segmented organs: domain/zone -> cell enrichment -> DEG/GO -> pathway score -> spatial marker validation.
- Fig. 5 links intestinal lipid handling and liver zonation. Dotplots summarize lipid absorption/metabolism genes, IF validates ileum lipid-related changes, and liver layer maps quantify Lpl/Zbtb20 expression around central/portal vein zones. The story function is to connect one tissue's epithelial function to another tissue's metabolic axis. For reuse, cross-organ mechanistic claims need matching spatial axes in both tissues.
- Fig. 6 closes with bile-acid multi-omics. It compares BA concentration distributions across body sites, composition proportions, classic/alternative pathway ratios, liver/intestine BA gene expression and a hepato-intestinal model. This figure supplies biochemical support for the transcriptomic metabolism story. For reuse, metabolite measurements can upgrade a spatial program into a physiology claim; without them, keep language at pathway-score level.

Open method modules:
- `cross_tissue_cell_atlas`: annotation module; unifies cell types across organs.
- `spatial_aggregation_index`: spatial statistics module; quantifies cell aggregation in tissue maps.
- `immune_compartment_shift`: differential_program module; tracks B/myeloid/NK/T changes across tissues.
- `intestinal_zonation_programs`: spatial_structure module; zones epithelial programs and cell enrichment.
- `metabolism_multiomics_link`: cross_dataset_comparison module; connects spatial programs to lipid/bile-acid measurements.

Reusable pattern:
- Cross-tissue papers should first identify global systems affected, then zoom into spatially interpretable organs and functions.
- Use for new data when: multiple tissues or organs are sampled under the same condition contrast.
- Do not overgeneralize from one tissue to whole-body claims.
